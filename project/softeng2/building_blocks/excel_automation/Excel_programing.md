## Outlook: The case of Box2D and Angry Birds

There's actually an interesting story behind this. At a GDC panel where Rovio was presenting about their multi-million-dollar franchise, Box2D's creator Erin Catto called them out for not giving him credit. A caught-off-guard Rovio exec said the company would credit Box2D if Catto would see him after the Q&A. [Hacker News](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=2273694)

For context, Box2D is a free open-source 2D physics simulator written in C by Erin Catto, published under the MIT license, and has been used in many well-known games including Angry Birds, Limbo, Tiny Wings, and Shovel Knight.

https://lib.ivank.net/?p=demos&d=box2D

**Why give it away for free?**

When software is free and widely adopted, the person who made it gains things that have indirect value:

- **Reputation** — Erin Catto is well-known and respected in the game dev world specifically _because_ of Box2D. That reputation helps his career (e.g., getting hired at Blizzard).
- **Influence** — He gets invited to speak at conferences like GDC, which further builds his standing.
- **Talent network** — People who learned physics programming through Box2D may later collaborate with him or hire him.

## Why excel? 

Microsoft Excel was first released in **1985**, initially for the Apple Macintosh. The Windows version followed in **1987**.

VBA (Visual Basic for Applications) was added to Excel in **1993**, with the release of **Excel 5.0**. This was also the version that introduced the modern tabbed worksheet interface. VBA replaced the older Excel 4.0 macro language and eventually became the standard automation language across all Microsoft Office applications.
 In 1982 Lotus 1-2-3, an electronic spreadsheet that became the best-selling personal-computer software of its day.
